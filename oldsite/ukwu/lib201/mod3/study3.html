After completing this module, you will know how to:

  • define your research topic
  • determine your information needs
  • locate and retrieve relevant information

Defining a Topic

Sometimes, the hardest part of research is deciding on a topic. Fortunately, there are a number of reference sources in the library that might inspire you with a suitable topic. CQ Researcher, and Issues and Controversies on File are two such works. They treat current, controversial topics, and they both have excellent indexes. After deciding on your topic, write it down as a search statement with at least two identifying concepts. You may need to narrow your topic. The following questions may help you accomplish this task and assist you in formulating a search query:

  • Is your topic brand new or has it been discussed over a number of years?
  • Is it a subdivision of a major field of knowledge?
  • Is your topic global or is it relevant only to the United States?
  • Is there a person, a group, or an institution involved in your topic?

Here is an example of a topic statement with a clearly defined point of view:

Animal testing is a necessary cost for the greater benefits of mankind.

Here is an example of the search process. Notice that the main concepts are identified first, followed by possible synonyms or related concepts, which you could build into your search statement.

Concept #1   Concept #2   Concept #3
animal testing AND benefits AND humanity

Here is a second example using synonyms or related concepts:

Concept #1   Concept #2   Concept #3
animal rights AND cost AND human lives

And here is a third example using synonyms or related concepts:

Concept #1   Concept #2   Concept #3
animal protection AND price AND human beings

Determining Information Needs

The scope and type of your assignment dictate your information needs. The following points may help you focus on the information you need to complete your research:

  • Type of assignment (e.g. short essay versus final project)
  • Currency of information (is this criterion relevant or irrelevant to your research?)
  • Type of information (overview versus in-depth study)
  • Type of publication (popular magazines versus scholarly journals)
  • Type of format (print, audio, electronic, etc.)
  • Viewpoint (neutral versus partisan viewpoints)

Locating and Retrieving Relevant Information

Once you have determined your information needs, the next step is to locate and retrieve relevant information, using appropriate resources. Libraries have excellent on-site and online resources for users' research needs. Over the coming weeks, we will learn about these reference resources and how to use them. Reference sources provide an excellent overview and background information on a particular subject. Information comes in various types (e.g., books and periodical articles) and formats (e.g., print and electronic). Depending on your information needs, you may only need to use reference works, in-depth books, or both books and periodical articles to complete your research paper. Internet resources are included as acceptable sources of information.
