Scope of the Collection

The Library collects primary and secondary publications, bibliographic and reference resources, and machine-readable materials in the format best able to support the educational and informational needs of Long Beach City College faculty, students, and community in all subjects relevant to the College curriculum and instructional programs. In some cases, specific policies govern particular types and formats of materials.

Formats and Coverage

Books/Monographs

Every member of the faculty is encouraged to participate in the important function of book selection, based on an understanding that the extension and upgrading of the book collection to reflect the needs of the curriculum can best be accomplished through the cooperative efforts of the faculty and the Library staff. Faculty requests for books may be submited online, to the Collection Development Librarian, or to School Liaisons. Department chairpersons who wish to receive copies of appropriate book reviews from such book selection tools as Choice should contact the Collection Development Librarian. If possible, faculty requests should be accompanied by priority designations, such as "first priority," or "second priority."

The Library obtains the majority of its books from a wholesale book service, Midwest. When a title is available from Midwest in a clothbound or paperback edition, the Library purchases the paperback edition, to reduce cost. Textbooks, workbooks, and study guides are only collected by exception.

Paperback books

A separate collection of mass-market and trade paperbacks is maintained to support the recreational and avocational demands of the students. Because of their high usage and fragility, paperbacks in this collection receive a minimal amount of processing consistent with inventory control and circulation in a browsing collection.

In some instances, paperback books are added to the cataloged collection. Consideration for such addtions include the following:

  1. The title is unobtainable in another format.
  2. The title is in current heavy demand.
  3. The price of the hardbound edition is excessive.
  4. The paperback edition is purchased as a duplicate copy.

Children's books

A separate collection of picture books is maintained to support the Child Development program and to encourage a love of reading in children. The collection reflects the multicultural population of the college community. It includes Caldecott award-winning books.

Career books

A separate collection of career-related books is maintained to support job exploration, job preparation, and career development. Many students seek an education to get a job, and in support of this goal, the Library strives to keep this collection current and up-to-date.

College catalogues

A separate collection of college catalogues and directories of educational institutions is maintained.

Plays (scripts)

Although a number of plays are incorporated in the cataloged collection, a separate collection of acting scripts is maintained to meet the demands of the Theater program. At least two copies of each script are purchased, and as in the case of paperback books, these materials receive a minimal amount of processing.

Periodicals

I. Introduction

The serials collection must be sufficient to support the educational and research programs of the college. Serials are publications issued in successive parts bearing numeric or chronological designations and intended to be continued indefinitely, such as periodicals, newspapers and annuals, standing orders, multi-volume sets issued in separate years. Formats for serials include print, non-print and electronic all of which will be considered in the library's purchase and/or access decisions.

The management of periodicals requires a higher degree of selectivity than that of books. Initiating a periodicals subscription implies an ongoing financial commitment in terms of payments, binding and storage . The selection of periodicals and decisions about specific titles, numbers of copies and locations are made by the Access Services & Electronic Resources Librarian. A librarian also has been assigned to each LBCC academic department and program on both campuses. Library liaisons will work with departments to help identify and facilitate cooperative opportunities for building the collection, weeding (see the weeding policies on p.5), compiling bibliographies, locating research materials, and disseminating information.

The annual subscription list is essentially pre-selected, the result of many decisions made over previous decades. The library provides representative periodicals in a wide range of subjects of reference value and recreational interest. Particular emphasis is given to titles included in periodical indexes. Specialized titles are considered in relation to subject needs of the faculty and students. The periodical collection is reviewed annually for additions and deletions. The library staff coordinates periodic reviews of titles of interest to the various departments to identify items that might be dropped and to gather suggestions for titles to be added. The results of these reviews are combined and analyzed by the Access Services & Electronic Resources Librarian and the library staff, and then reviews are used as a guide in deciding periodicals collection. A list of added and deleted periodical subscriptions is circulated to the faculty and the library staff for comment before changes are made.

The Periodicals department provides and maintains alphabetical, subject, and location holdings lists, both in print and online. The LAC Library maintains a subscription list of approximately 1,346 periodical titles. The PCC Library subscribes to approximately 480 titles. These lists are updated whenever cancellation or new subscriptions are added to the library collection, and are checked for currency annually.

Periodicals titles could also be searched through Voyager, the library catalog. The periodical records indicate which libraries (LAC & PCC) own a particular title and volume. Periodicals are generally retained for four years in hard copy format and daily newspapers are retained for three months. Back issues of serials are purchased only when deemed necessary or as the budget permits. Some titles for which indexing is available and for which there is heavy demand are maintained indefinitely in microform .

In addition to onsite periodical holdings, the library also provides access to full text articles through several online databases . Title lists for these databases are included in the general periodical holdings lists, above, but are also listed separately at the periodical desk.

 

A. Library Periodicals Funds Allocation

The LBCC libraries have a limited serials budget, which must be used to maintain two (2) campuses. Capital Outlay are requested every fiscal year to fund the renewal of subscriptions and newly recommended purchase of periodicals, electronic databases, and general services. In addition, computer hardware, software, maintenance services and other operating expenses are covered under existing budgets. The Vice President of the Academic Affairs and Dean of Academic Affairs is responsible for allocating the budget.

Serials inflation averages at least 20% annually. Because periodicals costs are rising at a much faster rate than books, every effort is made to hold the number of subscriptions to the minimum that is consistent with need. We also coordinate to trim our costs holdings as much as possible with the branch library. Therefore, serials selection and de-selection decisions are made with very careful consideration. Both campuses will subscribe to a title only in cases of popular use. Faculty members who wish to request the addition of a new subscription will be asked to review existing subscriptions in their subject discipline in order to identify a current title that might be discontinued to contain costs. Electronic journals that are free on the web are selected by the Librarians and added to the website by the Cataloging Librarian and Systems Librarian.

The Access Services & Electronic Resources Librarian together with Periodicals Department library staff reviews the usage figures and budget constraints every spring semester to determine which title becomes a candidate for non-renewal in the next fiscal year. The Librarian Liaisons will be involved in the process by being responsible for follow-up information once the Serials staff disseminates serial holdings information to faculty and with the departments affected via Department Heads/Deans. Recommendations for adding and/or dropping serials are welcomed and encouraged from faculty members.

B. Selection Process

  • the Access Services and Electronic Resources Librarian will actively pursue new subject areas and be aware of new periodical titles;
  • Librarians, library staff, and faculty review selection requests;
  • recommendations for new titles are encouraged from both students and faculty;
  • selection is managed on an annual basis because of the nature of subscriptions;
  • for reference titles, preference will be given to those magazines with index/abstract access;
  • preference will be given to the most appropriate format, electronic or print for new subscription titles, intended to be retained long-term. The initial subscription and any back issues may be purchased through the periodicals budget to ensure holdings from first issue.

C. Selection Criteria

The quantity and specific titles at any one campus library depend on a number of variables: course offerings, enrollment, faculty and student requests, library use, space and budgetary limitations. The goal is to provide basic coverage at most campus libraries to support specific courses being taught as well as to provide both general interest and news magazines of international, national and local interest.

The following criteria are used in evaluating titles for acquisition or cancellation:

  • curriculum support for students;
  • satisfy the educational, informational, and recreational needs of the College;
  • strength of the existing collection in the title's subject area;
  • present use of other periodicals in this subject area;
  • provide currency and projected future use;
  • cost;
  • reputation of journal and the publisher;
  • inclusion in a reliable indexing source;
  • number of recent interlibrary loan requests for this serial.; and
  • continued subscription contingent upon adequate.

Patron and staff suggestions for addition to the collection are evaluated by such standard selection tools: Ulrich's International Periodical Directory, Magazines for Libraries, ads and sample issues. A primary consideration for addition to the collection, in addition to cost, is whether the title is indexed in one of the Library's periodical databases.

D. Backfiles

a. Requests for back files of new subscriptions will be reviewed separately. Back files for newly initiated subscriptions will normally not be immediately acquired until use patterns of current issues have been established, as well as the need for back issues determined.

b. Missing back files of titles to which the library is subscribing will be acquired according the availability of funds and the evidence of need. When acquiring back files, the most preferred format will be electronic or microform.

c. Collection gaps are reviewed and acted upon individually based on user demand and availability of funds. Gifts, which do not duplicate the library's holdings , are welcome and are often used to satisfy collection gaps.

E. Gifts

The collection develops not only through purchase, but also through the receipt of gifts. These may consist of unsolicited as well as solicited donations of materials, monetary contributions, or free materials ordered by the Library. The Access Services & Electronic Resources Librarian

and the library staff at the Periodicals Department evaluates gifts in the same manner as purchased items and provided they meet the management collection criteria, and provided that there are no restrictions attached regarding their arrangement or disposition. Periodicals must be in good, clean and usable conditions.

Duplicate copies received, as gifts will be added only if the circulation record of the existing copy suggests a single copy is not sufficient to meet the demand or it needs to complete the holdings in the collection or if they can be used to replace worn copies. The Library does not appraise gifts

for tax purposes; however, the Library will send the donor an acknowledging letter, which will include a description of the gift.

F. Binding

Periodicals and journals are generally bound on a regular basis when funding is available. Binding is not necessary when the current two to three years issues are retained or microforms are purchased to replace print issues. The number of issues bound together is determined on a title-by-title basis depending on the size and number of issues per volume or year. As a general rule, incomplete volumes or years are not bound.

G.  Weeding

In order to maintain a vital collection of resources, the Library must continuously strive not only to selectively acquire appropriate materials, but also to remove items which no longer meet criteria for inclusion in the collection and which are not expected to be reasonably useful in the future.

The Recommendations for removal of materials should be forwarded to the Access Services & Electronic Resources Librarian who will coordinate the ongoing "weeding" process. Together with Librarian Liaisons and appropriate faculty, he/she will review the items recommended for withdrawal from the collection and ultimately arrive at a final decision. The following considerations relating to all types of format need to be thoughtfully examined during this process:

•  relevance to the existing curriculum;

•  little or no usage;

•  physical condition;

•  multiple copies;

•  availability at local libraries;

•  incomplete and short runs of title may be withdrawn, particularly when title is not received currently;

•  items where information currency is of the essence such as newsletters and trade magazines;

•  due to lack of space, issues which replaced by microform are routinely discarded;

•  replacement of the print with the electronic format;

•  lack of indexing in standard indexes.

It is recommended that titles be retained if included in the following category:

•  titles which are listed in standard bibliographies e.g., Ulrich's International Periodical Directory, Magazines for Libraries or are considered a core work in a subject field.

 

Newspaper subscriptions

Current print newspapers are acquired on a highly selective basis. Emphasis is placed on local newspapers, and then major U.S. newspapers representing regions of the country. Foreign newspapers are acquired on a highly selective basis. Priority is given to newspapers that are indexed. Their retention depends on availability of space, microform, and usage. Issues are retained for three months or until microfilm copy arrives. Newspapers are selected based on the following:

•  represent a variety of social, cultural, and political viewpoints;

•  provide instructional and research support;

•  provide current information;

•  provide sources of national and international news;

•  recreational reading; and

•  access to archives for historical research.

The Los Angeles Times and New York Times are purchased on microfilm and are retained for archival and research purposes.

The LBCC library also subscribes to newspaper electronically on the Web, via the ProQuest Direct Database : Los Angeles Times, New York Times, Wall Street Journal, Christian Science Monitor, Washington Post, and Historical Newspaper Los Angeles Times 1881-1986.

Microforms

The term "microforms" refers to microfilm and microfiche. Currently, the library subscribes to

two (2) newspapers on microfilm and two hundred twenty seven (227) titles on fiche as backup to the print copies or in place of the print copy as cost dictates over print materials. For ease of use, microfiche is the preferred format, rather than microfilm.

Microforms are added to the collection for one or more of the following reasons: (1) to acquire materials not available in print; (2) to compress space required to store materials; (3) to preserve materials in the process of degenerating;

(4) to serve as a backup copy of some of the high demand items; and

(5) to fill in gaps in the library's serials holdings.

The selector must consider the following factors when deciding whether print or microforms will be the favored medium:

•  potential use of the material;

•  how long the print format will last;

•  special feature that the microfilm does not provide, such as color illustrations;

•  original material is important;

•  store space;

•  equipment for reading and printing;

•  costs of various formats; and

•  if, in the judgment of Library staff, the paper copy would be subjected to mutilation.

 

Government documents

Long Beach City College Library is not designated as a depository library, and therefore, it orders Government-generated material. With the exception of continuations, emphasis will be placed on acquiring pamphlet material. Government publications that are purchased are subjected to the general guidelines and criteria. Government material of a technical nature will generally not be acquired.

Sound recordings

Sound recordings, including both music and spoken-word recordings, are collected and housed at the Circulation Desk as audiocassettes. At the time of the writing of this policy, the Library is evaluating the use of MP3 technology, stored on compact discs, as a more durable alternative to audiocassettes.

Video recordings

Video recordings, expected to be of significant long-term value in support of instruction, are collected. Videocassettes in this collection may be checked out to students for off-campus viewing. The preferred format is VHS. Priority for acquisition is given to titles requested by instructors in at least two disciplines to supplement classroom instruction and completion of assignments.

Compact discs

Compact discs, including both music and spoken-word recordings are available in an open collection available for browsing. Recordings requested by faculty members in support of teaching are given preference in acquisitions. The Library does not accept donations of duplicated copies of compact discs.

E-books

The Library recognizes the value of e-books in libraries and acknowledges their accessibility to students enrolled in online education. Under the existing budget, the Library does not have the funds to acquire an e-book collection at the consortial offering; however, the Library is currently seeking funds to initiate an e-book collection and views it as a coherent part of its overall policy.

DVDs

The Library does not currently collect DVDs but will consider starting a collection when DVD players are available to the faculty through Media Equipment Circulation and available to students in the Center for Learning Assistance Services.

Principal Areas of Library Collections

  1. Circulating collections. Circulating material is acquired according to need, authority, scope, cost, treatment, arrangement, and format.
  2. Reference. Reference resources are acquired according to need, authority, scope, cost, treatment, arrangement, and format.
  3. Reserve Material/Textbooks. The Library maintains a reserve area where reserve material is collected, organized, and circulated. The Library does not generally purchase copies of textbooks used in classes, unless the text is considered useful to the general College community, or if the material has already been acquired in the normal course of collecting for the Library. Instructors may supply classroom or text materials and place them on reserve for students. Instructors who make materials available through the reserve collection are responsible for complying with the applicable copyright laws, as stated in the amended Copyright Act of 1976.

Arrangements with Other Institutions for Resource-Sharing, Cooperative Collections

LBCC Library recognizes that it cannot provide everything that its customers request within its own collection of resources. Therefore, the Library participates in several resource-sharing programs. As a member of Online Computer Library Center Interlibrary Loan System (OCLC), the Libraries Very Interested in Sharing (LVIS), and with reciprocal borrowing arrangements with CSU Long Beach and Long Beach Public Libraries, LBCC is committed to the cooperation and sharing of resources among libraries and other agencies that acquire, house, and make information and materials accessible.

Interlibrary loan (ILL) serves as a means of providing access to specialized, out-of-print, and other materials the Library does not have in its collection. However, interlibrary-loan service is not a substitute for the development of adequate collections. In accordance with the National Interlibrary Loan Code, 2000, a member library should develop an adequate collection based on the needs of its service area and patrons. If a title is requested more than twice in a six-month period (and is still in print), it must be purchased.

The Library is committed to building a collection of resources that responds to and is capable of filling most of its customers' needs. The courtesy of resource-sharing and ILL is called upon only when the Library cannot satisfy the request with its resources. The decision to purchase or borrow an item is made by the Collection Development Librarian, Access Services Librarian, and ILL-delegated staff.

Because interlibrary loan is costly in terms of staff time for both borrowing and lending libraries, the Library may limit the number of requests an individual may make at a given time. The Library may levy a surcharge for requests that exceed a given number per year for an individual and for rush treatment of requests.

Although the Library absorbs most of the internal costs of interlibrary loan, including most database searches and the cost of staff time involved in the process, it may pass on to the requestor any charges assessed by the lending library. Such charges may include

  1. loan and photocopy charges,
  2. postage and handling fees,
  3. lost, damaged, or overdue fees.

LBCC Library also complies with all provisions of the U.S. Copyright Law (17 U.S.C.) and its amendments. The Library supports the Fair Use section of the Copyright Law (17 U.S.C. §107), which permits and protects citizens' rights to reproduce and make use of copyrighted works for the purposes of teaching, scholarship, and research.

For interlibrary loan borrowing and lending policies and procedures, please go to http://lib.lbcc.edu/services/ill.html.

Collection Development is the intellectual process of matching current needs with the physical collection, adding materials where necessary, and weeding materials no longer important to the collection.
