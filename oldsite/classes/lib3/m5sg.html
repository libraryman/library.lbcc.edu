Indexes

An index is a pointer to a piece of information in periodicals or in books. An index does not contain the information itself. An index is arranged in a certain order designed to guide users to the needed piece of information, be it an article in a periodical or a chapter in a book. The goal of indexes is to guide users to the intellectual content and the physical location of documents.

There are two types of indexes:

  • Book indexes
  • Periodical indexes

Book indexes


A book index provides a pointer to the information included in the book; a book index is arranged alphabetically, gives a page location, and is usually found at the back of the book. Example of a book index such as a travel book would include place names, historical events or historical sites, hotels, and resorts. Example of a book index such as a cook book would include ingredient names, recipes, measurements and culinary activities.

Before going further, you should first become familiar with the following terms:

A periodical is a publication with a fixed interval between issues or numbers, such as weekly, monthly, or quarterly. Examples of periodicals are Newsweek, Business Week, and National Review.

A journal is a publication dealing with a specialized subject, containing articles written by experts in the field. Journals tend to be scholarly in nature and research-oriented, where the result of primary research is reported. The articles published in journals frequently have abstracts ­i.e., summaries- and bibliographies. Examples of journals are American Journal of Psychology and Journal of the American Medical Association.

A magazine is a popular publication of general interest, which is usually written in non-technical terms and frequently has illustrations. Examples are National Geographic and Sports Illustrated.

Refer to Module 4 on evaluating information to distinguish between a popular magazine and a scholarly journal.

Periodical indexes


Periodical indexes are much different than book index. A book generally is written by one author on a specific topic with a single focus from the beginning to the end. In contrast, a periodical is open-ended, with contributions by different authors using different styles writing on different subjects. From this sea of information, indexes must create some order and provide a clear and standardized arrangement to guide users to the right document. There are two types of periodical indexes:

  • Individual indexes
  • Broad indexes

An individual index would only index for a specific periodical, for example: National Geographic Index would cover only articles from National Geographic magazine, Los Angeles Times Index would cover only articles from the Los Angeles Times.

Broad indexes would index articles from thousands of periodicals. There are general broad indexes (e.g. Readers' Guide to Periodical Literature), and discipline-specific broad indexes (e.g. Business Periodicals Index).

Periodical indexes are published in various formats: print, microforms, CD-ROM, online. In this LIB 1 course, we will concentrate on printed and online periodical indexes.

Print periodical indexes

The Liberal Arts Campus Library subscribes to several print periodical indexes, which are published by the H.W.Wilson Company. Print indexes are updated monthly and sent to libraries in soft cover editions. At the end of a twelve-month period, they are bound in an annual volume with the corresponding year clearly marked on the spine.

Print indexes are arranged alphabetically by author and subject headings with subdivisions. Users will search under headings to locate relevant periodical articles; each article listing is called a citation. One main disadvantage of using print indexes is that users still need to go to the periodical shelves and look for the article in the correct periodical, which sometimes is not available due to loss or being checked out. The main advantage with print indexes is that users can search for retrospective information published many decades ago. For example, the New York Times Index goes as far back as 1851.

Since the form, arrangement, and frequency of these indexes are similar, only one index will be described in detail below:

Readers' Guide to Periodical Literature

This index covers approximately 190 general-interest periodicals, such as Time, Ebony, Scientific American, and People Weekly. The arrangement is by specific subjects in one alphabet. There are many cross references. Under the subject, each entry lists articles by title, author, name of the periodical (in italics and usually abbreviated). volume, pages, and date.

 

Electronic periodical indexes

Electronic indexes are updated almost daily and offer choices of searches not available in print indexes. Users can choose unstructured keyword search, or publication search, or a structured advanced search within specific date range. Electronic indexes offer a one-stop-shop experience in that users obtain not only citations, but also abstracts and full-text of articles with printing, downloading, and e-mailing capabilities. The main disadvantage with electronic indexes is that most of their databases begin from 1986 only. So, if users want to find retrospective periodical articles published before 1986, they have to use print indexes. Specialized indexes

Granger's Index to Poetry

This index has been around for a long time, and it will help you locate poems in collections. Each volume is organized in the same way, comprised of several sections. In the back of the volume, there is a subject index and an author index. The major section is a title and first line index in one alphabet. Each entry lists the anthologies in which the poem appears. All of the books indexed are listed in the front of the volume. If an anthology is available at the Liberal Arts Campus Library, the call number is pencilled in next to the title.

Short Story Index

These volumes index short stories in collections. The main section lists short story titles, authors, and subjects in one alphabet. The author entries list every short story by that author and the anthology where each one can be found. The anthologies are listed in the back of each volume. If the anthology is available in the Liberal Arts Campus Library, the call number is written next to the title entry.

Essay and General Literature Index

Every year, essays are published in collections on many subjects. This index lists these essays by authors, about authors, and about individual works of authors. In addition, it indexes essays by subject. Each entry lists the author of the essay and its title, lists the books it is in, as well as the page numbers. The books indexed are listed in the back and those owned by LBCC Library have the call number written by the title.

Play Index

Play index is published every five year. It indexes plays that were published during a given five-year period. Plays which were omitted from the previous volume would be included in the current volume. In the word of the publisher, each volume "contains both separately published plays and plays in collections, written in or translated into English. Its broad scope ­ from puppet plays to classical drama ­ and detailed cast analysis make Play Index suitable for a variety of uses." It is a combined title, author, and subject index arranged alphabetically.
