Definitions


  • Information literacy is a set of abilities requiring individuals to "recognize when information is needed and have the ability to locate, evaluate, and use effectively the needed information."


  • American Library Association. Presidential Committee on Information Literacy. Final Report.
  • Chicago: American Library Association, 1989 http://www.ala.org/acrl/nili/ilit1st.html

Why is it important to teach Information Literacy?
  • Develop lifelong learning skills
  • Computer literacy vs. information literacy
  • Develop critical thinking skills
  • Cogent and well articulated papers
  • Interconnectedness and global nature of information
  • Informed decision making
  • Prepare students for transfer to 4-year colleges
8
What are the skills needed to be information literate?
  • Recognize when information is needed
  • Know where and how to discover information needed
  • Comfortable using a variety of tools to find, modify and assimilate information
  • Critically evaluate and synthesize information
9
Who is responsible for information literacy?



  • Shared responsibility of all educators and information providers
  • Collaboration of teaching faculty and librarians ensures success
  • Course-integrated sessions provide valuable reinforcement of skills
10
Importance of integrating IL into the curriculum
  • IL is a process not a separate set of skills
  • Integrating IL into the curriculum reinforces skills and techniques
  • The student can utilize the skills learned immediately
  • Skills can be utilized and transferred to other classes
11
Methods of teaching IL
  • Resource based learning
  • Active Learning
  • Team-based learning
  • Exercises that develop critical thinking skills
  • All these require collaboration between teaching faculty and librarians!
12
Techniques for teaching IL
  • Emphasizes inquiry approach to learning
  • Higher order tasks (analysis, evaluation, synthesis, and reflection)
  • Motivates students to participate
  • Learning emphasizes teamwork over individual performance
  • Accommodates different learning styles
  • Professor acts as facilitator

Some quick remedies/fixes

  • Schedule an instruction session with library faculty
  • Bring your students to the library or      explore librarys  Home Page
15
Better remedies!
  • Integrate library research into course curriculum
  • First task: contact a librarian to discuss  designing/redesigning your syllabus to incorporate library research.
  • Timing is everything - a library session just before students start to research a topic is ideal!

17
Some basic principles



  • Incorporate a variety of information types & formats to show students the strengths & weaknesses of different formats ( Internet, books, journals, magazines, etc.)
  •  Be clear about level of research you expect scholarly vs. popular
  • Are data or statistics required?
18
Pitfalls to avoid!


  • Avoid scavenger/treasure hunts that just make students look for an answer  - they only test librarians ability to find unrelated bits of trivia.
  • Groups of students seeking the same factoids doesnt teach them critical thinking.
19
More pitfalls to avoid!
  • Assigning same topic to all students - library materials are gone unless you put them on reserve
  • Too narrow a topic for which the library only has a few items
  • Limiting the students to particular journals/time periods
  • Assuming students prior knowledge/experience

Designing an assignment
